# Capability Domain

## Purpose

This document defines Capabilities as reusable enterprise skills.

Capabilities are the reusable asset at the center of the CommandCore operating model.

## Responsibilities

- Preserve reusable operational, technical, research, or workflow value.
- Allow companies and projects to consume repeatable enterprise skills.
- Provide a bridge between innovation intake and durable enterprise reuse.
- Support evaluation, review, ownership, and future marketplace readiness.

## Ownership

Capabilities are owned by CommandCore architecture and governed through the Living Capability Library and Capability Reviews.

An individual capability should also have a designated owning role, team, or executive attention area.

## Lifecycle

Lifecycle pattern:
1. A useful pattern, tool, workflow, runbook, or component is discovered.
2. It is reviewed inside Innovation Lab or a project context.
3. It is scored for reuse, value, effort, risk, and fit.
4. Review outcome becomes Adopt, Adapt, Watch, or Reject.
5. If promoted, it enters the Living Capability Library.
6. Companies and projects consume it over time.

## Relationships

- Capabilities may originate from Projects, Innovation Lab items, research, tools, runbooks, workflows, or agent skills.
- Companies consume capabilities.
- Projects may produce or consume capabilities.
- Agents may use capabilities during execution.
- Capability Reviews, Technology Radar, and Project Incubator are adjacent domains.

## Future Extensions

- Formal capability dependency maps
- Capability certification workflows
- Capability maturity scoring
- Capability usage analytics
- Shared capability libraries across company worlds
- Marketplace packaging and publication

## Examples

Examples from the locked blueprint:
- Local project runbook template
- Access map pattern
- JSON backup/export
- Universal search index
- Client handover pack
- PDF splitting
- HEIC to JPG conversion
- Long-context knowledge retrieval
- Agent task interface

## Rules

- CommandCore collects capabilities, not just projects.
- Products and companies consume capabilities.
- Capability value must be reusable beyond one project whenever possible.
- Capability evaluation must consider risk, effort, dependency, lock-in, and strategic value.
- Sprint 1 documents the future Capability entity even if it is not fully implemented.

## Canonical Capability Facets

### Versioning

Versioning distinguishes meaningful revisions of a capability's definition, maturity, or approved form.

The locked documents require replaceability and reviewability, which logically imply explicit version awareness even before implementation is formalized.

### Ownership

Ownership identifies the role, team, or executive area accountable for the capability's quality and evolution.

### Dependencies

Dependencies are the other capabilities, tools, providers, infrastructure services, or runbooks a capability requires.

### Inputs

Inputs are the context, records, artifacts, or systems required to apply the capability.

### Outputs

Outputs are the reusable results the capability produces, such as artifacts, workflows, transformed records, or operational outcomes.

### Documentation

Documentation is the human-readable explanation of what the capability is, why it exists, how it is used, and where it came from.

### Tests

Tests are the verification activities that show whether the capability works as expected in its intended scope.

The blueprint does not prescribe implementation tests, but it does require reviewability, safe execution, and practical usefulness.

### Certification

Certification is the formal approval state indicating the capability is trusted for use by companies, projects, or agents.

### Marketplace Readiness

Marketplace Readiness indicates whether a capability is sufficiently documented, packaged, governed, and reusable to be shared or sold beyond its original origin.
